# Nurundere — Taplin (1879), verbatim

*Quelle: George Taplin, „The Narrinyeri", in: J. D. Woods (Hg.), *The Native Tribes of South
Australia*, Adelaide 1879, S. 55–58. **Gemeinfrei** (Digitalisat: [Internet
Archive](https://archive.org/details/nativetribessou00benngoog), Roh-OCR unter
`quellen/taplin-native-tribes-1879_djvu.txt`).*

> **Doppelte Vermittlung — vor jedem Zitat mitlesen.** Dies ist **nicht** die Ngarrindjeri-Stimme,
> sondern ein **Missionstext**: Taplin nennt Nurundere „the Supreme Being", „a god", deutet ihn
> euhemeristisch als „a deified chief" und nennt die Überlieferungen „ridiculous traditions". Der
> christlich-viktorianische Rahmen ist Teil der Quelle, nicht der Erzählung. Außerdem ist es **eine**
> Fassung von mehreren (vgl. Berndt/Karloan 1939–40, mit Karta/Kangaroo Island stärker) — es gibt
> *kein* einziges „Original". Schreibung der Namen original belassen; offensichtliche OCR-Fehler
> still korrigiert (Nunindere/Nunmdere → Nurundere, NepeUe → Nepelle u. a.).

---

## Nurundere als Stifter (S. 55)

> The Narrinyeri call the Supreme Being by two names, Nurundere and Martummere. He is said to have
> made all things on the earth, and to have given to men the weapons of war and hunting. Nurundere
> instituted all the rites and ceremonies which are practised by the Aborigines, whether connected
> with life or death. On inquiring why they adhere to any custom, the reply is, because Nurundere
> commanded it.

> Although the natives say that Nurundere made all things, and that he now lives in Wyirrewarre, yet
> they tell many [„ridiculous", Taplins Wort] traditions about his doings when he inhabited the
> earth … He is represented to have been a great hunter, and there were contemporary with him two
> other remarkable hunters named Nepelle and Wyungare.

## Der geteilte Fisch — Ursprung der Fischarten (S. 56)

> Once upon a time, it is said, that Nurundere and Nepelle together pursued an enormous fish in Lake
> Alexandrina, near Tipping. Nepelle caught it, then Nurundere tore it in pieces, and threw the
> fragments into the water, and each piece became a fish, and thus ponde, tarke, tukkeri, and
> pommere, different kinds of fish, had their origin. But another sort of fish, tinuwarre (called
> bream by the whites), was produced in a different manner. Nurundere went to Tulurrug, and there
> finding some flat stones, he threw them into the Lake, and they became tinuwarre.

*Fußnote Taplins:* eine andere Fassung — *Nurundere and his sons drove this great fish down the
Darling and Murray to a place called Piltangk in Lake Alexandrina, and there obtained assistance
from Nepelle to catch it.*

## Wyungare, die zwei Frauen, das Feuer, der Aufstieg (S. 56–57)

> Wyungare was a personage who had no father but only a mother … and was a mighty hunter of
> kangaroos. Once he was amongst the reeds at Oulawar … and Nepelle's two wives passing by saw him,
> admired his handsome form, and fell in love with him … they … begged him to take them for his
> wives, to which he willingly consented. Of course Nepelle was very angry … he put fire in the hut,
> and told it to wait until Wyungare and the two women slept, and then to get up and burn them.

> … the fire pursued them. For miles they ran along the shore of the Lake, chased by the vengeful
> element, until they reached Lowanyeri, plunged themselves in the mud of the swamp there, and the
> fire was unable to reach them. Afraid of the implacable hatred displayed by Nepelle, Wyungare
> sought a means of escape. He determined to effect this by going up to Wyirrewarre to live there.
> So he tied a line to a spear, and hurled it at the heavens … he pulled himself up, and afterwards
> the two women. Three stars are still pointed out as Wyungare and his wives.

> Before his ascent it is related that he took a gigantic kangaroo and tore it in pieces, and
> scattered the fragments through the scrub, and they became the comparatively small kangaroos which
> exist now.

## Nurunderes Frauen, die Flut, die Verfolgung, der Aufstieg (S. 57–58)

> To return to the adventures of Nurundere. He had four children by his two wives. Once when he
> dwelt at Tulurrug two of his children strayed away into the scrub … and were lost. Soon afterwards
> his two wives ran away from him. He pursued them … to Encounter Bay, and there, seeing them at a
> distance, he exclaimed in anger, "Let the waters arise and drown them." So the waters arose in a
> terrible flood … and, overtaking the fugitives, they were overwhelmed and drowned. … the dense
> part of the milky way is said to be the canoe of Nepelle floating in the heavens.

> Then Nurundere went up the Coorong in search of his two lost children. At Salt Creek he met with a
> blackfellow … they fought. He speared Nurundere in the thigh; but he laughed at the wound … and in
> return speared the blackfellow through the body and killed him. Afterwards Nurundere … found his
> two lost children …

> After these things Nurundere went to Wyirrewarre, taking his children with him. The Narrinyeri
> always mention his name with reverence. I never heard them use it lightly or with levity … The
> natives regard thunder as the angry voice of Nurundere, and the rainbow as also a production of
> his.

> The legends of Nurundere are fast fading from the memory of the Aborigines. The young people know
> very little about them, and it is only from the old people that the particulars of them can be
> obtained.
